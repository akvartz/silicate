On reading surfaces

A note on building the page as if it were a book, not a product.

The browser's defaults are ninety percent right. We fix line length, leading, and contrast — and stop. The reader's eye should land on the words, not the chrome.

For most of the web's history, the answer to how should this page look? has been: like a product. Cards, shadows, gradients, fixed headers, animated reveals on scroll. None of these are wrong, exactly. They are simply off-target when the goal is to be read.

The measure

The single most consequential design decision in long-form typography is the measure: how wide a line of text is allowed to grow before it wraps. The classical rule is sixty to seventy-five characters; we settle on about seventy.

Anything you can do, you can do at thirty-four rem.

silicate.css

Below that and the eye flicks back too often. Above it and the return-sweep loses the next line. Seventy is the number that disappears.

What the variables look like

:root {
  --silicate-measure: 34rem;       /* ~70ch */
  --silicate-leading: 1.55;
  --silicate-accent:  #8a4b2a;
}

Override these at :root and you have rethemed the system. There is nothing else.

What we leave out

  • No shadows. Anywhere.
  • No rounded corners. Sharp by default.
  • No gradients. Backgrounds are flat.
  • No emoji. Use typographic marks.
  • No theme toggle. Your machine knows whether it is night.

Frequently raised objections

What if I need a sidebar?

Then you are building something silicate is not for. That is fine. Pick a different system; this one will not contort itself to fit.

What about dark mode?

It is built in and switches with the OS. There is no toggle. The reader's machine already knows whether it is night.

How do I add a brand color?

Override --silicate-accent at :root. That is the brand color.
